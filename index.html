Could your roof be a power plant?

Arlington's government already runs on renewable electricity, mostly from a big solar farm downstate. The next phase is rooftops: yours, your condo's, your congregation's. None of this requires becoming an electrician. It requires knowing which door to knock on.

  • Join a group-buy round. Capital Area Solar Switch pools Northern Virginia buyers for group pricing. Free to join, no obligation to purchase.
  • Book a free energy coach. The county's AIRE Home Energy Hub offers free virtual coaching sessions and rebates for efficiency and electrification upgrades.
  • Electrify on replacement. When the gas furnace or water heater dies, that's the cheap moment to switch to a heat pump. Plan it before it dies, not after.

What goes in which cart?

Arlington changed the rules twice in recent years: glass left the blue cart in 2019, and food scraps joined the green cart in 2021. Most sorting mistakes trace back to those two changes, so here is the current version.

Blue cart

curbside recycling
  • Paper and cardboard, clean and dry, boxes flattened
  • Metal cans and rinsed aluminum
  • Plastic bottles, jugs, and tubs, rinsed, lids on
  • No glass. It has its own system now, see the purple bins
  • No bags, and never bag your recyclables. Loose only

Green cart

food scraps + yard waste
  • All food scraps since 2021: produce, coffee grounds, and yes, meat, bones, and dairy!
  • Leaves, brush, grass, same cart, same truck
  • Paper bags or certified liners keep the cart livable
  • Where it goes: an industrial composting facility in Manassas
  • What comes back: finished compost for parks, gardens, and residents

Neither cart

special handling
  • Glass goes to the purple drop-off bins and gets crushed into sand and gravel
  • Batteries and electronics go to E-CARE, the county's spring and fall event at Wakefield High School
  • Paint and chemicals, year-round drop-off at the Trades Center
  • Plastic film and bags return to grocery store bins
  • Clothing and textiles to donation bins, not the blue cart

Three schools make their own power

Discovery Elementary opened in 2015 as the largest net zero elementary school in the country, and the first school anywhere to earn LEED Zero Energy certification. Fleet and Cardinal followed. The interesting part is what happens inside: the dashboards are wired into math and science class, so the building itself is the textbook.

  • Rooftop solar plus geothermal. Discovery pairs about 1,700 panels with wells under the play field.
  • Live data in class. Kids graph their own school's production against its load. Kilowatts become homework!
  • Green teams move the meter. Student energy patrols and recycling crews, coached well, produce measurable savings.

What can you do in our community to make a difference?

Three ways in, all real, all local! Follow a road or just read on.

At home

  • Get the carts right, it takes one week of habit
  • Book a free AIRE energy coach session
  • Join a Solar Switch group-buy round
  • Swap gas appliances when they die, on your terms

Around town

  • Tree plantings, pushing canopy from 35% toward the 40% goal
  • Four Mile Run stream cleanups
  • EcoAction Arlington volunteer days
  • E-CARE drop-offs every spring and fall

In the room

  • County Board hearings and Community Energy Plan updates
  • Civic association energy committees
  • School green teams and the APS sustainability committee
  • Public comment is open to everyone, use it
